About KW GeeseCam

Hydrocat/GeeseCam Project

Motivation

The motivation for the project was to be able to see and find out the trail conditions in the Hydrocut without having to actually go into the forest. While we can make assumptions about what the trails are like, we really can't know for sure. The project got started when one of the developers obtained a 4G modem from Worldine Canada for testing. Worldline is introducing a new 4G service.

Technology

Solar Charger

The solar charger is based on a 20W Colman solar panel from KW Surplus driving a Chinese charge controller picked up from Amazon. It's not the greatest setup, but the upgrade to an improved solar charge controller seems to allow the battery to be maintained.

The battery is a 12V deep cycle gel cell battery. The system runs on 12V.

Website

This website is a static html page based on a theme purchased from Themeforest. The camera calls a REST service on the web host to upload the image data (filename, annotation, etc.) to a MySQL database. The same REST service is called by javascript code in the backend of the website to update the pages for the website background images as well as the gallery links. Images are collected from the camera and uploaded via FTP to the web host for storage.

Camera

The camera is based in a Raspberry PI W connected to a Raspberry camera. To that board is a one wire temperature sensor and a I2C light sensor. The light sensors allows the Raspberry PI to detect the amount of light and adjust the camera's exposure. An I2C voltage sensor is used to check the battery voltage. Battery, temperature, and light information is posted to our ThinkSpeak ChannelWe want to know when the battery is getting to low and needs some help. We've had the SD card in the Raspberry PI get chewed up when the battery drops out. The voltage sensor allows Raspberry PI to text us when there is a low voltage condition and shutdown the system before the solar charge controller pulls the power to the device.

Future Direction: Step 1 - Remote Sensors

Future versions will be enhanced with remote RF sensor units that will collect temperature, humidity, and soil moisture readings. Using these LORA radio sensors, we will be able to determine if the trails are muddy or not. We will allow be looking into infrared sensors to detect mud and ice directly for winter trail conditions.

Future Direction: Step 2 - Artificial Intelligence

The purpose of the camera images is more than photos of the trails. We would like to be able to count the number of riders in the Hydrocut using only imaging technology. The camera system will be updated to use motion detection to sense riders in the forest and snap their photo. The images collected by the motion detection system will be fed into TensorFlow to train the AI to detect a ride, a hiker, wildlife, etc. and get an accurate count of the entities in the forest.

Why Geese?

Currently, the solar charger and the camera hardware in development. While we had the camera in the forest for a few weeks, it was a pain to hike 30 mins into the forest to make repairs and adjustments. The camera now resides in a team mambers backyard where geese congigate. It makes for an easy place to get at when things fail or stop working. We can practice using images to train our AI system using Geese.

The Hydrocut Webcam

For the first version, we bought some parts from KW Surplus, and hiked into the forest at the Hydrocut to mount the camera and solar charger up at one of the trailheads.

This first version ran off an old car battery and a cheap solar panel. It managed to last about two weeks before the battery died and the Raspberry PI gave up.

Geese Webcam

The second version used an improved gel cell battery and was moved to one of the co-developers back yard to make getting at the device easier - no 30 min hike into the forest.

The unit lasted about a week again before the battery died. The unit is currently being upgraded and in the meantime the solar charge controller was replaced with a better model. The battery appears to be lasting.
